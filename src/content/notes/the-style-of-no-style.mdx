Idea from Bruce Lee. Also reflected here: 

<BlockQuote cite="https://gwern.net/unseeing#atoms" author="Gwern Branwen" work="On Seeing Through and Unseeing: The Hacker Mindset" medium="essay">
In stealth games⁠, players learn to unsee levels into patterns of gaps moving around over time—gaps in guard patrols or observability of light/sound—and how to dismantle the level piece by piece until they can go anywhere and do anything.
</BlockQuote>

And here:

<BlockQuote cite="https://www.henrikkarlsson.xyz/p/hacker-mindset" author="Henrik Karlsson" work="How to walk through walls" medium="essay">
Most systems can be viewed at multiple levels. There is a superficial system which pretends to be made of one thing (walls, hens). But actually, it is really made of something else (bits, memory allocations). And if you learn to understand that underlying system, you can find ways to use the lower-level details to steer the system in a way that looks incomprehensible to those who only see the more superficial system.
</BlockQuote>

<BlockQuote cite="https://www.henrikkarlsson.xyz/p/hacker-mindset" author="Henrik Karlsson" work="How to walk through walls" medium="essay">
What we’re talking about here isn’t like going to school—it emphatically is not that—suffering through all of the boring prerequisites before you get to do the exciting parts. What we’re talking about is actually doing the fun stuff, playing around with projects that excite you, trusting that you can learn enough to solve your problems. If you keep tinkering, doing one fun project after another, you will eventually see through the system.
</BlockQuote>

<BlockQuote cite="https://www.henrikkarlsson.xyz/p/going-your-own-way" author="Henrik Karlsson, paraphrasing Jacob Collier" work="Some relationships deepen when you tell the truth and some end" medium="essay">
There are no “wrong notes” you can play in a song: you just have to figure out how to bring them home—how to resolve the notes in a way that makes sense with the overall music.
</BlockQuote>

It's looking past complexity to the fundamental dynamic.

Likewise, photographers try to reduce a scene to an arrangement of lines and shades.

When I dance, I aspire to reduce the music to whatever the musical equivalents of lines and shades are.
